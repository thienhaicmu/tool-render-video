The lighthouse had been dark for forty years when Elias took the keeper's job. The village called it cursed; he called it quiet. On his first night, climbing the spiral stairs with a lantern, he found the old logbook of the previous keeper, Mara, its final entry torn out.

For weeks Elias tended the restored lamp, its beam sweeping the black water. But every night at 3:07, the light would flicker and dim on its own, as if something passed between it and the sea. He began to hear a voice in the wind — a woman, calling a name that was not his.

One storm-lashed evening, a ship's horn wailed offshore, lost in the fog. Elias raced to the lamp, but it died completely. In the darkness he saw her: Mara, pale and translucent, standing at the glass. She had drowned that night forty years ago, unable to light the lamp in time, and had kept vigil ever since — trapped by her failure.

Elias understood. He took her spectral hand and together they turned the great lens by force, coaxing a spark back into the wick. The beam blazed out through the storm. On the water, the ship's horn answered — grateful, alive — and steered safe past the rocks.

At dawn the lamp still burned. Mara was gone, her vigil finally ended. Elias sat on the gallery, the sea calm and silver, and for the first time the lighthouse felt not cursed, but free.
